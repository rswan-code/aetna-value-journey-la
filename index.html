Members respond.

The foundation of any value case is whether members show up. In Louisiana, they are — at a rate that meaningfully exceeds what the prior model ever produced.

On November 1, 2025, the Aetna BH Louisiana contract changed. Not the budget — the budget stayed the same at $136,591 per year. What changed was the structure. Where the old model charged Aetna a flat rate at contract signing, the new model charges only when members are genuinely engaged. It is an alignment of incentives that changed what both sides are accountable for.

Part 2 of 5

Members engage.

Reaching a member once is not engagement. What the new model produces is sustained conversation — and the difference between 14-minute calls and 26-minute calls is not a data point. It's a relationship.

The flat-rate model averaged 1.7 calls per member and 14 minutes per call. That is enough to check a box. It is not enough to build the trust required for a Medicaid maternal health member to disclose that she hasn't seen an OB in four months, or that she doesn't understand her insurance, or that she is scared and grieving. The new model produces something different.

Two structural changes drive the engagement improvement. First, the onboarding workflow now leads with understanding the member's needs — before app download, before registration, before any administrative step. That shift earns trust at first contact. Members share more because someone asked first. Second, the care team is staffed for phone-based depth — appointment scheduling, barrier navigation, and care coordination — not just companionship. These are conversations that accomplish something. Longer calls are a symptom of that.

On the engagement rate this month: The April engagement rate reflects a moderation as the program manages pace relative to budget. This is expected at the 75% mark of a contract year — and it does not diminish the engagement built in the prior months, which is now converting into the care outcomes shown in Part 4.

Part 3 of 5

Engagement deepens — and uncovers what was hidden.

The same screening questions were asked in the old model and the new. What changed is how deeply members engage — and therefore how much they're willing to disclose. The data below shows what that difference looks like.

In Louisiana, the new engagement model identified more needs in 3 of 13 screened SDOH categories. Social and community isolation — the dominant need in this population — rose from 79% to 83%. Housing stability identification increased from 41% to 44%. These are not massive jumps, and the Louisiana market is one where further calibration may deepen the gains. But the direction is clear: more sustained engagement surfaces more of what matters.

Two findings from the deeper engagement are worth calling out specifically. First, 61% of screened members flagged an urgent need — meaning they are in active distress, not just chronic need. Each urgent flag triggers an outbound call within one business day. These are members who might not call their plan. But they do pick up when someone calls them. Second, 44% of members score as lonely on the UCLA-3 loneliness assessment, and 84% of those lonely members also show psychological distress (PHQ-4 ≥3). This is the high-utilization cohort that drives disproportionate plan spend — and they are being identified and actively supported through 8,912 loneliness interventions delivered via the Pyxir platform.

Why this matters: Loneliness and psychological distress are among the strongest predictors of high healthcare utilization in Medicaid populations. Identifying and measuring these members — not just engaging them once — is what the sustained engagement model enables. The flat-rate model couldn't get deep enough to find them consistently.

At 75% of Budget · April 2026

You have a choice to make. Here is our honest assessment of each option.

Per our agreement, we wanted to proactively bring you a clear picture of where we are — in performance and in investment — so we can align on the right path together. The engagement is translating into action. The return will build as that engagement continues to convert into sustained care outcomes. At this inflection point, there are three paths.

Pyx Recommends
Option 1 — Continue at current pace and scope

Maintain the current population and engagement level. This allows the care team to continue building on the relationships that are already producing outcomes — the 31% prenatal/postpartum completion rate, the 108 postpartum follow-ups, the 68 prenatal visits. The infrastructure is in place. The engagement is in motion. Continuity at this level delivers the largest potential return and allows the value that is already building to fully compound over the remaining months of the contract year.

Also Viable
Option 2 — Refine scope, maintain engagement quality

Concentrate on the highest-impact member cohorts — High Cost / High Utilization (4.2% of the eligible population) and members with the most complex barrier profiles — while maintaining the same depth of engagement. This concentrates resources where the return on engagement is highest and where engagement is most likely to directly convert into quality outcomes and cost avoidance. The engagement model's effectiveness is preserved; the scope is narrowed to where it matters most.

Pyx Advises Against
Option 3 — Maintain scope, reduce engagement intensity

Reach the same members with fewer or shorter interactions. Our concern here is direct: the value in this program is realized through depth, not reach. The 31% prenatal and postpartum completion rate does not happen because a member was called once. It happens because a care team was present across multiple conversations — long enough to earn trust, surface barriers, and support action. Reducing engagement intensity limits the mechanism. If this option is selected, we would expect a meaningful reduction in care outcome rates in subsequent reporting periods.

Our recommendation: Continue the current approach (Option 1), or concentrate scope if needed (Option 2). Both allow us to build on the momentum already in motion and convert the engagement built over the past five months into the maternal outcomes Aetna is accountable for. The proof of concept is established. The opportunity is to let it compound.
